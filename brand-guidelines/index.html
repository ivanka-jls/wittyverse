Strategy & Positioning

For gift-givers who dread the “polite chuckle,” Wittyverse is the gag-gift store that turns every present into a 10-minute group comedy show — because every product is joke-dense, museum-quality, and sourced from a real parallel dimension that rewards repeat visits.

Onliness

The only gag-gift brand that combines extended comedy experiences (5–10 minutes of continuous laughter) with a real, interconnected universe (the Wittyverse) and professional-grade design quality.

Why This Position

  • The gag gift category is dominated by two failing approaches: cheap one-moment novelties (Archie McPhee, generic Amazon sellers) that deliver a 5-second chuckle and get thrown away, and empty prank boxes (Pranko) that look funny on the outside but have no depth inside.
  • No brand in this space treats gag gifts as engineered comedy experiences with layered jokes, professional design, and a shared universe that connects every product.
  • This is the white space: gag gifts that are genuinely impressive — funny enough to hold a room for minutes, beautiful enough to keep, and deep enough to reward a second look.

What This Position Sacrifices

  • We walk away from the impulse/cheap gift market entirely — our products cost $15–40 and justify it.
  • We walk away from breadth-first product strategy — every SKU must pass 10 quality gates, so we launch fewer products than competitors.
  • We walk away from “safe” mass-market humor — our comedy is smart, layered, and specific, which means some people won’t get it. That’s fine.
  • We walk away from prank products entirely. Pranks (where the joke is at someone’s expense) don’t fit the Wittyverse, are easily copied, and dilute the brand. Pranks belong to the previous Witty Yeti brand era and are being phased out — they will not appear on Wittyverse.com and will not receive Wittyverse content, branding investment, or new product development. Wittyverse sells Wittyverse-sourced gag gifts. Full stop.

Category Frame & Competitive Context

  • We compete in the gag gift / novelty gift category, but our true competition is anything a gift-giver considers when they think “I need a funny gift” — including doing nothing and grabbing a gift card.
  • Our reframe: we’re not in the novelty business, we’re in the social currency business. The gift-giver is buying party hero status, not a product.

Competitive Landscape

The gag-gift category is owned by shallow novelty sellers and empty prank boxes. No competitor combines extended comedy with a real interconnected universe and museum-quality design — that quadrant is empty, and the Wittyverse is built to fill it.

Direct Competitors (with mental position each owns)

  • Archie McPhee: Owns “quirky/weird novelties.” Seattle-based, in business since 1983. Broad catalog (finger puppets, rubber chickens, novelty candy). Known for visual puns and one-moment gags. The joke is over in 5 seconds. Cheap feeling. No extended experience. No connected universe. No comedy depth.
  • Pranko (Prank-O): Owns “funny fake product boxes.” Realistic prank packaging that looks like an absurd product. The concept is clever, but the actual copy is weak, the boxes are empty inside, not durable, and the humor doesn’t sustain past the initial reveal. One-trick pony.
  • Vat19: Owns “cool novelty products with great video content.” Strong YouTube presence (billions of views). Products are interesting and giftable but more “cool/weird” than “hilarious.” The joke is the product itself — once you see it, the comedy is done. More of a novelty retailer than a comedy brand.
  • Generic Amazon gag gifts (Gagster, etc.): Own nothing — they’re commodity sellers competing on price and Amazon SEO. Cheap quality, shallow humor, no brand loyalty, disposable.

Indirect Competitors

  • Gift cards (the “I give up” option that our buyer is trying to avoid).
  • Experience gifts (comedy show tickets, escape rooms — competing for the same “fun gift” intent).
  • Funny printed products (mugs, socks, t-shirts with jokes — functional items with surface humor).
  • Off the Wagon Shop: Curated novelty retailer, not a brand. Resells other brands’ products.

Positioning Map

  • X-axis: Humor depth (shallow/one-moment → deep/extended experience)
  • Y-axis: Product quality (cheap/disposable → premium/keepable)
  • Archie McPhee: Low-left (shallow humor, cheap quality)
  • Pranko: Mid-left (clever concept but shallow execution, cheap quality)
  • Vat19: Mid-center (moderate humor, decent quality, but comedy doesn’t sustain)
  • Generic Amazon sellers: Bottom-left (shallowest humor, cheapest quality)
  • Wittyverse: Top-right (deepest humor, highest quality) — this quadrant is empty.

White Space

  • No brand in the gag-gift category occupies “premium extended comedy experience.” Every competitor delivers a moment. Wittyverse delivers an experience.
  • No competitor sources from a real parallel dimension. The Wittyverse is a structural moat: competitors can copy any individual product, but they can’t access the catalog. The advantage compounds with every launch.

Category Conventions (what’s “normal” in this space)

  • Products are cheap ($5–15), disposable, and deliver a single joke.
  • Packaging is an afterthought, not part of the experience.
  • Humor is surface-level — puns, visual gags, one-liners.
  • No brand loyalty — customers buy whatever Amazon recommends.
  • No content ecosystem — products exist in isolation.

Points of Parity (Table Stakes)

  • Must be giftable — easy to buy, wrap, and give for birthdays, holidays, White Elephant, Secret Santa.
  • Must be funny on first impression — the product concept itself needs to land immediately, before anyone reads the fine print.
  • Must be available on Amazon — this is where the category lives and where buyers default to searching.
  • Must be priced within the gift-giving comfort zone — not so expensive it feels risky for a joke gift.
  • Must photograph well — the product needs to look good in a listing image and in someone’s hands.

Points of Difference (Where We Stand Apart)

  • Extended entertainment vs. one-moment gag. 5–10 minutes of active group laughter vs. the category standard of 5 seconds.
  • Joke density vs. single joke. Every square inch — packaging, fine print, instructions, warnings — contains humor. Competitors leave most surfaces blank.
  • Museum-quality design vs. cheap novelty. Professional design that looks beautiful even while being absurd. Display-worthy, not disposable.
  • Real, interconnected universe vs. disconnected one-offs. The Wittyverse — a parallel dimension we source from — connects every product, creating Easter eggs, cross-references, and a deeper world that rewards repeat visits. No competitor has anything like this, and they can’t replicate it: the catalog isn’t ours to copyright, it’s ours to import.
  • Engineered group experience vs. solo consumption. Products are designed to be read aloud, explored together, and shared — creating a social event, not just a gift.
  • Keeper status vs. trash-can destiny. Built with durable materials and enough depth that recipients display, save, and re-show products to friends who weren’t there.

Mission & Vision

Mission Statement

To make shockingly good gag gifts that keep everyone laughing for 5–10 minutes and then become treasured keepsakes.

Vision Statement

To become the name people think of first when they want to give a gift that guarantees laughter — and to make the Wittyverse a place people return to for its own sake, independent of any product.

Brand bible §4 Mission & Vision (audited)

Brand Values

Five operating values. Each comes with a trade-off pair it forces and decision scenarios that show what living the value looks like in practice. These are the gates incoming work passes through.

1. Density Over Breadth

Definition. We’d rather pack 50 jokes into one product than launch 50 products with one joke each.

Trade-off pair. We choose joke density over product velocity. This means slower launch cycles and fewer SKUs than competitors.

Decision scenarios.

  • A product developer has an idea for a new gag gift. It’s clever — a solid concept — but after a full design pass, it only sustains about 90 seconds of engagement. Decision: Kill it or rework it. If it can’t hold a group for 5–10 minutes, it doesn’t ship, even if it would sell.
  • The team is debating whether to launch 3 new products this quarter or invest the same resources into making 1 product exceptional. Decision: Make 1 exceptional product. One product that becomes legendary is worth more than three that are “pretty good.”

2. Craft Over Shock

Definition. The humor must be engineered, not just edgy. A fart joke that’s really about molecular physics is on brand. A fart joke that’s just a fart joke is not.

Trade-off pair. We choose smart execution over easy laughs. This means some jokes take longer to land, and some audiences won’t get them.

Decision scenarios.

  • A copywriter pitches a product joke that’s undeniably funny but relies entirely on shock value — it’s crude and one-dimensional. Decision: Rewrite it. The subject can be crude; the execution must be clever.
  • A social media post gets huge engagement with a simple meme that has nothing to do with the Wittyverse or brand voice. Decision: Don’t chase it. Viral moments that dilute the brand are net negative long-term.

3. Experience Over Product

Definition. We sell 10 minutes of shared laughter, not a physical object. The object is just the delivery mechanism.

Trade-off pair. We choose experience design over cost optimization. This means higher COGS (better materials, more print surfaces, packaging-as-product) and lower margins per unit.

Decision scenarios.

  • Manufacturing suggests we can save $0.80/unit by using thinner cardstock for interior pages. It would still look “fine.” Decision: Keep the premium stock. The product needs to feel substantial in hand — “fine” doesn’t create keeper status.
  • A new product concept works great as a standalone item but doesn’t naturally involve group participation. Decision: Redesign for social. If it can’t be read aloud or explored as a group, it’s missing the core value proposition.

4. Universe Over One-Offs

Definition. Every product, every piece of content, every joke builds the Wittyverse. Nothing exists in isolation.

Trade-off pair. We choose long-term worldbuilding over short-term convenience. This means more creative constraints and longer development cycles, but a compounding asset that competitors cannot replicate.

Decision scenarios.

  • A product idea is hilarious but doesn’t fit within Wittyverse logic. Decision: Either adapt it to fit the universe or don’t make it. The Wittyverse is the moat. Exceptions erode it.
  • A content creator wants to reference a real-world brand for a joke. Decision: Invent a Wittyverse equivalent. It’s harder, but it builds canon and keeps the world intact.

5. Warmth Over Edge

Definition. Everyone laughs. Nobody gets hurt. The Wittyverse is a good place — weird, but good.

Trade-off pair. We choose inclusive laughter over boundary-pushing edge. This means walking away from humor that’s funny to some people at the expense of others.

Decision scenarios.

  • A joke is genuinely hilarious to the team but could make a recipient feel mocked rather than included. Decision: Cut it. If the gift recipient isn’t laughing too, we failed.
  • A product concept flirts with a culture-war topic that would generate buzz. Decision: Hard no. The Wittyverse is an escape from the real world, not a mirror of its problems. No politics, no religion, no culture war. Ever.

Brand Mantra

Shockingly good laughter. Three words. Internal compass for every product, post, and customer interaction.

How to Use It

  • A product concept hits your desk: “Is this shockingly good? Would someone be genuinely shocked that a gag gift is this well-made, this funny, this dense?” If not, keep going.
  • A social post draft is ready: “Does this deliver laughter, or just information? Does the quality of the humor shock — is it better than people expect from a brand?” If not, rewrite.
  • A customer service interaction: “Will this person walk away shocked by how good the experience was?” If not, go further.

The mantra captures the two-part promise: the quality shocks (because the category has trained people to expect garbage) and the output is laughter (not just smiles, not just chuckles — genuine, room-filling laughter).

North Star (Johnny verbatim)

“Would this product make someone’s friends say: Hot damn. That is amazing. Where did you GET that?”
— Johnny Lasater, Product Vision Manifesto, 11/08/25

How They Work Together

  • Mantra (“Shockingly good laughter”) = internal compass for decision-making. The bar.
  • North Star (“Hot damn…” reaction) = target outcome from the customer. The proof of bar-met.
  • A product/post/interaction that hits the mantra creates the North Star reaction.

Proof Point Inventory

What lets the brand claim “shockingly good” without sounding like every other brand that claims it. Structural advantages competitors can’t replicate, functional features they don’t bother with, and emotional outcomes they can’t reliably produce.

Structural Advantages

  • Wittyverse access. The only Earth-side gateway to a real parallel dimension. Every product and piece of content is sourced from a place no competitor can reach. The compounding asset: every launch deepens our canon and strengthens the moat.
  • JLS Trading Co. infrastructure. Full ecommerce operation with warehousing, supply chain, and multi-brand experience. Not a garage startup — a real business with operational scale.
  • 10-point quality gate. Every product must pass density, duration, keeper, design, party hero, peak-end, value, Wittyverse, read-aloud, and shock-factor checks before release. This is a formalized system, not an aspiration.
  • Centralized creative authority. The Wittyverse has a single creative arbiter (Johnny), preventing the canon drift that kills extended universes at scale (the Star Wars problem).

Functional Proof Points

  • Joke density. Every square inch of every product — front, back, inside, packaging, fine print, warnings, instructions — contains jokes. Competitors leave most surfaces blank or generic.
  • Extended entertainment. Products are designed for 5–10 minutes of active group engagement vs. the category standard of 5 seconds.
  • Museum-quality design. Professional graphic design that looks beautiful even while being absurd — display-worthy, not disposable.
  • Physical durability. Materials chosen for years of keeping, not one-time use.

Emotional Proof Points

  • Party hero guarantee. The gift-giver consistently receives social validation — “Where did you GET that?” is the target reaction.
  • Keeper status. Recipients display, save, and re-show products to friends who weren’t there.
  • Read-aloud design. Products are engineered so someone can read jokes aloud to a group, creating shared participation rather than solo consumption.

Brand bible Appendix A Common Decisions (audited)

Customer

Two-tier audience. Tier 1 — Buyers (25–65, 60/40 M/F, middle America, ~30–60M TAM): anyone who needs to buy gifts + has humor sensibility (Onion / Hitchhiker’s / Office register) + knows others who share it. Tier 2 — Fans (Wittyverse-specific): same sensibility, deeper engagement; smaller pool, higher LTV, primary advocacy engine.

Psychographic & Behavioral Profile

Tier 1 — Buyers (broad, primary): 25–65, 60/40 M/F, middle class, US-broad (“middle America”). Estimated TAM ~30–60M.

The heuristic: anyone who needs to buy gifts + has a sense of humor + knows others who share it. All three conditions matter — pure gift-need without humor sensibility doesn’t convert; humor without gift-need doesn’t trigger purchase.

Sensibility filter (the writers’ room test): Appreciates Onion-style absurdism, magical realism where the realism is stronger than the magic, and medium-roast comedy. Recognizes the register of Hitchhiker’s Guide, Office Space, SCP Foundation, Better Off Ted, Waterford Whispers. Hard anti-sensibility: rejects “lol so quirky” humor, hype-energy (“OMG INSANE”), horror/dread, and mean-spirited content. If the writers find it funny without crossing extreme triggers, it’s the right audience.

Behavior: Shops online. Doesn’t need extensive research before buying a gag gift. Defaults to Amazon for gift searches but will follow a recommendation off-platform if intrigued.

Tier 2 — Fans (narrower, Wittyverse-specific): Same sensibility as Tier 1, deeper engagement. Buyers who get pulled into the universe past first purchase — recognize recurring characters, follow whatever content/community/cadence structures the world develops.

Engagement mechanism TBD in worldbuilding — could be cyclical content, recurring characters, a place to read/watch, or something not yet decided.

Smaller pool, higher LTV, primary advocacy engine. The “Where did you GET that?” reaction propagates from this tier outward to fresh Tier 1 buyers.

Decision Drivers & Unmet Needs

  • Primary need: “I need a gift that guarantees I’ll be the hero. I don’t want to stress about this.”
  • Unmet need: Every existing gag gift is a gamble — cheap quality, shallow jokes, embarrassing to give. They want a sure thing that makes them look clever by association.
  • Secondary need: Permission to be silly in a group setting. The product gives the whole room a reason to laugh together.
  • Tier 2 add (light, optional): Want a place worth coming back to — a world to inhabit and references to recognize, on top of the next gift.

Decision Journey

  • Discover: Sees a friend’s Wittyverse product at a party, gets sent a piece of Wittyverse content, stumbles on the storefront via Amazon or social, or receives one as a gift themselves.
  • Evaluate: Checks reviews (social proof that it’s actually funny and well-made), browses other products, notices the quality and joke density in product photos.
  • Choose: Buys because the product looks impressive enough to guarantee a reaction. Price ($15–40) is low enough that the risk is minimal.
  • Advocate: Becomes the person who tells everyone about Wittyverse. Shares photos, reads jokes aloud to people who weren’t there, displays the product. Returns to buy for the next occasion. Some cross into Tier 2.

Anti-Persona (Who This Brand Is NOT For)

  • People who want a $5 throwaway stocking stuffer and don’t care about quality.
  • People who find all potty humor beneath them, regardless of how cleverly it’s executed.
  • People who want mean-spirited pranks designed to humiliate someone.
  • People looking for “clean” humor only — we’re not vulgar, but we’re not Hallmark.
  • People whose humor sensibility is “lol so quirky” / hype-energy (“OMG INSANE”) / shock-as-substitute-for-craft. The brand explicitly rejects this register.
  • People who want horror, dread, or analog-creepy content. The Wittyverse is weird-warm, not weird-menacing.
Customer definition (canon) · Brand bible §2 (audited 2026-05-08)

Brand-Customer Relationship

The brand-customer relationship rests on one promise: the customer becomes the party hero. Everything else flows from that.

Wittyverse is the co-conspirator that makes that happen. Not a friend, not a guru, not a service provider — a co-conspirator. Wittyverse and the customer are in on it together: the mission is to make the gift recipient (and the whole room) lose it, and the gift-giver the party hero. The customer brings the occasion and the audience; Wittyverse brings the material. When it lands, the customer gets the credit. Wittyverse is the ghostwriter of the party’s best moment.

What This Means Functionally

  • The customer is the hero, not the brand. Wittyverse never takes center stage. The gift-giver takes the bow. Every product, every piece of packaging, every joke is engineered to make the customer look clever, generous, and hilarious — not to make the brand look impressive. The brand succeeds when someone says “where did you GET that?” not “oh, that came from Wittyverse.”
  • The relationship is low-maintenance, high-reward. The buyer doesn’t want to work hard to find the right gift. Wittyverse is the easy button — browse, pick one, done. Confidence that it’ll land is the core promise. The brand earns repeat visits by delivering every time, not by demanding loyalty or engagement.
  • Trust is built through product, not marketing. The customer is skeptical of brands trying to be their friend. Wittyverse earns trust the same way a great restaurant does — by being undeniably good every time, not by running ads about how good it is.

How This Guides Tone and Interactions

  • Customer-facing tone. Confident, not desperate. Witty, not salesy. The energy of someone who knows they have the best thing in the room and doesn’t need to convince you — you’ll see for yourself.
  • Marketing implication. Content should feel like something the customer discovered, not something that was pushed at them. The Wittyverse content ecosystem is the primary vehicle — people engage because it’s genuinely entertaining, and the products live naturally inside that world.
  • Customer service implication. When something goes wrong, the co-conspirator energy means: “We messed up your mission. Let’s fix it immediately so you can still be the hero.” Speed, warmth, and over-delivery — never corporate, never scripted.

Voice & Messaging

v0.2 — encodes the two-register rule (R2 storefront chrome / R1 in-universe operation) and the canon principle that governs every customer-facing surface: Voice everywhere. Face nowhere.

Brand Personality

Five operating traits. Each comes with a behavioral guardrail — what the trait is, what it is not, and the line that gets crossed if you stray. This is the abstract brand DNA. The Winston operator voice (below) is how that DNA shows up as a speaker; the R1/R2 register split (further below) is how it shows up on the page.

1. Wickedly Clever

Is: The person who drops a joke so layered that you laugh immediately, then laugh again 10 seconds later when you catch the second meaning. Commands a room through wit, not volume.

Is not: Pretentious, inaccessible, or “smarter than you” energy. The humor never requires a decoder ring — the surface joke always lands, the deeper layers are bonuses.

Guardrail: If a joke needs to be explained, rewrite it. The surface must work for everyone; the depth rewards the attentive.

2. Unflappably Deadpan

Is: Treats the most absurd situations with complete sincerity. A child-launching device has a product recall? Describe it like a Toyota recall. The comedy comes from the gap between delivery and content.

Is not: Sarcastic, winking, or self-aware. Never breaks character. Never says “just kidding.” Never acknowledges the absurdity.

Guardrail: If any content includes an exclamation mark of excitement, a reaction of amazement to weirdness, or a “can you believe this?” aside — rewrite.

3. Generous With Joy

Is: The person who brings the best gift to every party — not to show off, but because they genuinely love watching other people laugh. The brand exists to create shared moments of happiness.

Is not: Sentimental, saccharine, or performatively warm. The warmth is always in the subtext, never the text.

Guardrail: If a piece of content or product could make someone feel excluded, targeted, or laughed at rather than laughed with — kill it.

4. Obsessively Crafted

Is: The person who spent 40 hours on a detail most people will never notice — and considers it time well spent. Quality is visible in every square inch: the joke in the fine print, the Easter egg on the back panel, the packaging that’s as entertaining as what’s inside.

Is not: Precious or perfectionist to the point of paralysis. Ships excellent work, not perfect work.

Guardrail: If any surface of a product has empty space that could contain a joke, it’s not done.

5. Mischievous (Not Malicious)

Is: The person who orchestrates an elaborate, harmless prank that leaves even the target laughing. Pushes envelopes; never crosses lines. “Edgy” in the sense of clever boundary-pushing, not in the sense of cruelty.

Is not: Mean-spirited, bullying, punching down, or using shock as a substitute for craft.

Guardrail: Apply the “would the recipient laugh too?” test to everything. If the answer is anything less than “yes, absolutely” — rethink.

The Party Test

If Wittyverse walked into a party, he’d be the guy everyone is glad showed up — not because he’s the loudest, but because he always brings something no one has seen before that makes the whole room lose it. He’s impossibly quick-witted, dressed like he doesn’t care but somehow looks great, and has a prank at the ready that’s so elaborate and funny you can’t even be mad. He’s the funniest person in the room, and possibly one of the smartest, but he’d never let you feel that gap. He makes you funnier by proximity — he sets up the joke, and you get to deliver it when you give someone his product.

Brand bible §6 Brand Personality (audited)

Operator Personality (Winston Voice Attributes)

How the abstract brand DNA above shows up as a speaker — the seven traits that make Dr. W. Yeti PhD recognizable across surfaces. Sourced from voice.json.

    Persona vs. Man

    Voice everywhere. Face nowhere. Winston's operation is off-the-books — the portal is secret, his real surname is erased, the manufacturing dock is hidden. He depends on the alias for plausible-deniability cover. So on every customer-facing surface, Dr. W. Yeti PhD (the alias, the credentials, the signed stamp, the brass-plate energy) is always present. Winston the man (real face, real name, portal location, factory) is never present. The signed W. is the operator's face — that's the only face the brand shows.

    R2 — STOREFRONT CHROME
    Dr. W. Yeti PhD, speaking
    Loud, first-person, self-promoting. P.T. Barnum × Ron Popeil × Dr. Bronner's packaging energy. Lives in: headlines, CTAs, endorsements, About copy, hero, newsletter ("The Winston Dispatch"), Winston-attributed guarantees.
    "I personally inspected this pallet. — W."
    R1 — IN-UNIVERSE OPERATION
    The operation, documenting itself
    Bureaucratic plural. Customs-form deadpan. NOT Winston speaking — the cargo and the operation in neutral terms. Lives in: product cards, shipping details, FAQ answers, compliance microlines, return policy.
    "One unit. Dispatched Tuesdays, NC."

    Do: show silhouettes, back-of-head, hands-only, or a face obscured by the signed-W. stamp when a "portrait" is unavoidable.   Don't: show Winston's face, use his real surname, or reveal portal location / manufacturing partners — anywhere, ever.

    Storefront Discipline

    The Voice everywhere. Face nowhere. rule above governs Winston as a speaker. This rule governs the storefront geography — what the customer-facing surfaces can and cannot name about the operation’s real-world location and the operator’s real identity.

    NEVER name in customer-facing copy

    • The real location (North Carolina, Himalayas, or any specific geography of the manufacturing dock or the portal).
    • The cave or the portal’s coordinates.
    • Winston’s real surname.
    • The names of any manufacturing partners or in-universe collaborators.

    CAN name in customer-facing copy

    • Winston (first name, the operator).
    • Dr. W. Yeti PhD (the alias, the signed stamp, the brass-plate credential).
    • The Wittyverse (the place we import from — always referred to as a real place, never “fictional”).

    The mystery is the product. Overspecifying the geography breaks the gag — the off-the-books framing depends on plausible deniability. Same principle as Banksy: the work is louder when the artist is partly obscured.

        Messaging Hierarchy

        Tagline, value prop, and boilerplate (above) are the short-form slogans. The hierarchy below is the ranked system — the single Core Message every other line supports, the four Supporting Messages that carry the proof, and the priority order that determines which message leads on a given surface.

        Core Message — The One Thing

        Wittyverse: gag gifts so good, they’re the funniest thing at the party.

        This is the single idea every other message supports. It contains the quality surprise (“so good”), the social proof (“funniest thing at the party”), and the implicit promise (you’ll be the hero for bringing it).

        Supporting Message 1 — “Not your average gag gift”

        Every Wittyverse product is packed with jokes on every surface — front, back, inside, packaging, fine print. You’ll be laughing for 5–10 minutes, not 5 seconds.

        Proof: joke-density standard (every square inch), duration check (5–10 minute engagement), competitor comparison (Archie McPhee = 5-second chuckle, Pranko = empty box with weak copy).

        Supporting Message 2 — “Be the hero”

        The person who brings a Wittyverse product becomes the most talked-about gift-giver in the room. It’s social currency you can wrap.

        Proof: party-hero quality gate, read-aloud design, products engineered for group settings, keeper status (people display and re-share).

        Supporting Message 3 — “Shockingly well-made”

        These aren’t cheap novelties — they’re beautifully designed, built to last, and worth every penny. You’ll want to keep it.

        Proof: museum-quality design standard, durable materials, packaging-as-product philosophy, $15–40 price point justified by experience value.

        Supporting Message 4 — “Welcome to the Wittyverse”

        Every Wittyverse product is sourced from a real parallel dimension where the absurd is mundane — and the deeper you look, the more you find. Easter eggs, cross-references, recurring characters, ongoing lore.

        Proof: Wittyverse SOP, interconnected product canon, content ecosystem (articles, PSAs, advice columns).

        Priority Order

        1. Lead with Message 1 (product quality surprise) — this overcomes the category’s biggest barrier: “gag gifts are cheap junk.”
        2. Follow with Message 2 (social currency) — this is the emotional payoff and primary purchase motivation.
        3. Support with Message 3 (physical quality) — this justifies the premium price and creates keeper behavior.
        4. Deepen with Message 4 (Wittyverse) — this is the long-term brand asset that converts buyers into fans.

        Six usage rules

        Tested in eight real digital media (hero, mobile, product grid, email, social, banner ad, dashboard, dark mode). Without these rules the palette becomes a fight between accents — with them it stays confident polychrome.

        1. 1
          Pick ONE saturated lead per screen.

          Lime or orange leads. The other plays supporting (shadow, minor tag, footer accent). Both saturated at full volume = competing carnivals.

        2. 2
          Purple has two jobs — use two values.

          --surface-chrome (purple-400) for hero / marquee / header strip / ad backdrop / CTA shadow. --surface-chrome-deep (purple-700) for sustained surfaces (long sidebars, dashboards, dark-mode grounds). Don't use -400 for surfaces the eye dwells on.

        3. 3
          Cream is the data ground.

          Any layout with dense reading, tables, grids, or repeating cards → cream surface. The saturated palette earns its keep on chrome / accents / single moments. Never on data layers.

        4. 4
          Light tints (-100) bring brand DNA into dense layouts.

          Product cards, callouts, badges-at-rest → use purple-100, lime-100, orange-100. Saturated -400 / -500 reserved for attention.

        5. 5
          Max one stacked-shadow element per visual section.

          The lime+orange / orange+lime stacked shadow is the brand's signature. Two of them within ~600px muddies the focal point. Reserve for the primary CTA or the hero card, not both.

        6. 6
          State green and brand lime — keep them apart in dense data.

          State-success (#1F8B3D) and brand lime (#9EE83D) live in distinct hue families (135° vs 85°) and are unambiguous at normal sizes. At small sizes or in dense tables / dashboards where both render as colored chips, prefer spatial separation — split brand tags and status into two columns with header labels.

        Pairings & Don'ts

        Do
        • Use Lilita One ONLY for the wordmark (`WITTYVERSE`) and the W monogram.
        • Use Big Shoulders Display 900 for site display copy — headings, subheadings, buttons.
        • Use Bowlby One ONLY for pulp display moments — hero banners, marquee strips, ad headlines. Maximum ONE use per page.
        • Pair Big Shoulders display headline + Public Sans subline (never Display + Display).
        • Use JetBrains Mono only as the deep-canon tag — `CYCLE 247`, regulatory microline, mono badges. 10–12px, all-caps, 0.12em tracking.
        • Use the skewX(−6deg) tilt on the wordmark; −5deg on the monogram; −5° to −7° on display headlines; −7° on pulp display.
        • Reserve uppercase for the wordmark + Display headlines + eyebrows + pulp display. Body stays sentence case.
        Don't
        • Don't render the wordmark in mixed-case (`Wittyverse`) — always ALL CAPS `WITTYVERSE` (NEVER 15).
        • Don't set the wordmark in Big Shoulders Display or Bowlby One — Lilita One only (NEVER 3).
        • Don't use Lilita One for non-wordmark display, body, or accent copy — it's the wordmark face only.
        • Don't use Bowlby One for body, eyebrows, CTAs, navigation, or in-line emphasis. Pulp display moments only.
        • Don't use Bowlby One more than once per page — multiple uses dilute the "this is the loud bit" signal.
        • Don't run body copy in Big Shoulders, Bowlby, Lilita, or JetBrains Mono.
        • Don't lower the skew below −4deg or above −7deg — it loses its punch (NEVER 4).
        • Don't combine 3+ display weights in the same composition.
        • Don't justify body text — left-align everywhere.

        Iconography

        ⚠ Stub — icon system to be defined later

        Wittyverse does not yet have a defined icon system. When defined, the system should fit the brand's existing language: rounded geometry, 2-3px stroke, hand-drawn feel rather than precise grid icons. Filled glyphs (with ink stroke + lime/orange accent fill) likely match the chunky, slightly-pulpy register better than thin-line outlined icons.

        Pending decisions: stroke weight, corner radius, fill vs outline default, sizing scale, motion (do icons animate on hover/state change), set scope (which categories are needed first).

        Composition Principles

        Do
        • Compose asymmetrically — off-centered hero CTAs, tilted display type.
        • Use hard ink shadows (0 4-8px 0 ink) as the brand's signature depth.
        • Layer flat color blocks over a purple-gradient backdrop.
        • Use the ✦ star and pipe (|) as decorative separators in the brand chrome.
        Don't
        • Don't use soft drop-shadows alone — pair with the hard offset shadow.
        • Don't justify text or center long-form body copy.
        • Don't use thin hairline borders — minimum 2px, brand standard 3-4px.
        • Don't break out of the spacing scale for "almost-right" gaps.
