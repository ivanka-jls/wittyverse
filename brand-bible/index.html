§1 Positioning

Positioning Statement

  • For gift-givers who dread the "polite chuckle," Wittyverse is the gag-gift store that turns every present into a 10-minute group comedy show because every product is joke-dense, museum-quality, and sourced from a real parallel dimension that rewards repeat visits.

Onliness Statement

  • The only gag-gift brand that combines extended comedy experiences (5–10 minutes of continuous laughter) with a real, interconnected universe (the Wittyverse) and professional-grade design quality.

Why This Position

  • The gag gift category is dominated by two failing approaches: cheap one-moment novelties (Archie McPhee, generic Amazon sellers) that deliver a 5-second chuckle and get thrown away, and empty prank boxes (Pranko) that look funny on the outside but have no depth inside.
  • No brand in this space treats gag gifts as engineered comedy experiences with layered jokes, professional design, and a shared universe that connects every product.
  • This is the white space: gag gifts that are genuinely impressive — funny enough to hold a room for minutes, beautiful enough to keep, and deep enough to reward a second look.

What This Position Sacrifices

  • We walk away from the impulse/cheap gift market entirely — our products cost $15-40 and justify it.
  • We walk away from breadth-first product strategy — every SKU must pass 10 quality gates, so we launch fewer products than competitors.
  • We walk away from "safe" mass-market humor — our comedy is smart, layered, and specific, which means some people won't get it. That's fine.
  • We walk away from prank products entirely. Pranks (where the joke is at someone's expense) don't fit the Wittyverse, are easily copied, and dilute the brand. Pranks belong to the previous Witty Yeti brand era and are being phased out — they will not appear on Wittyverse.com and will not receive Wittyverse content, branding investment, or new product development. Wittyverse sells Wittyverse-sourced gag gifts. Full stop.

Category Frame & Competitive Context

  • We compete in the gag gift / novelty gift category, but our true competition is anything a gift-giver considers when they think "I need a funny gift" — including doing nothing and grabbing a gift card.
  • Our reframe: we're not in the novelty business, we're in the social currency business. The gift-giver is buying party hero status, not a product.

§2 Target Customer

Psychographic & Behavioral Profile Tier 1 — Buyers (broad, primary): 25–65, 60/40 M/F, middle class, US-broad ("middle America"). Estimated TAM ~30–60M.

The heuristic: anyone who needs to buy gifts + has a sense of humor + knows others who share it. All three conditions matter — pure gift-need without humor sensibility doesn't convert; humor without gift-need doesn't trigger purchase.

Sensibility filter (the writers' room test): Appreciates Onion-style absurdism, magical realism where the realism is stronger than the magic, and medium-roast comedy. Recognizes the register of Hitchhiker's Guide, Office Space, SCP Foundation, Better Off Ted, Waterford Whispers. Hard anti-sensibility: rejects "lol so quirky" humor, hype-energy ("OMG INSANE"), horror/dread, and mean-spirited content. If the writers find it funny without crossing extreme triggers, it's the right audience.

Behavior: Shops online. Doesn't need extensive research before buying a gag gift. Defaults to Amazon for gift searches but will follow a recommendation off-platform if intrigued.

Tier 2 — Fans (narrower, Wittyverse-specific): Same sensibility as Tier 1, deeper engagement. Buyers who get pulled into the universe past first purchase — recognize recurring characters, follow whatever content/community/cadence structures the world develops.

Engagement mechanism TBD in worldbuilding — could be cyclical content, recurring characters, a place to read/watch, or something not yet decided.

Smaller pool, higher LTV, primary advocacy engine. The "Where did you GET that?" reaction propagates from this tier outward to fresh Tier 1 buyers.

Decision Drivers & Unmet Needs

  • Primary need: "I need a gift that guarantees I'll be the hero. I don't want to stress about this."
  • Unmet need: Every existing gag gift is a gamble — cheap quality, shallow jokes, embarrassing to give. They want a sure thing that makes them look clever by association.
  • Secondary need: Permission to be silly in a group setting. The product gives the whole room a reason to laugh together.
  • Tier 2 add (light, optional): Want a place worth coming back to — a world to inhabit and references to recognize, on top of the next gift.

Decision Journey

  • Discover: Sees a friend's Wittyverse product at a party, gets sent a piece of Wittyverse content, stumbles on the storefront via Amazon or social, or receives one as a gift themselves.
  • Evaluate: Checks reviews (social proof that it's actually funny and well-made), browses other products, notices the quality and joke density in product photos.
  • Choose: Buys because the product looks impressive enough to guarantee a reaction. Price ($15-40) is low enough that the risk is minimal.
  • Advocate: Becomes the person who tells everyone about Wittyverse. Shares photos, reads jokes aloud to people who weren't there, displays the product. Returns to buy for the next occasion. Some cross into Tier 2.

Anti-Persona (Who This Brand Is NOT For)

  • People who want a $5 throwaway stocking stuffer and don't care about quality.
  • People who find all potty humor beneath them, regardless of how cleverly it's executed.
  • People who want mean-spirited pranks designed to humiliate someone.
  • People looking for "clean" humor only — we're not vulgar, but we're not Hallmark.
  • People whose humor sensibility is "lol so quirky" / hype-energy ("OMG INSANE") / shock-as-substitute-for-craft. The brand explicitly rejects this register.
  • People who want horror, dread, or analog-creepy content. The Wittyverse is weird-warm, not weird-menacing.

§3 Competitive Landscape

Direct Competitors (with mental position each owns)

  • Archie McPhee: Owns "quirky/weird novelties." Seattle-based, in business since 1983. Broad catalog (finger puppets, rubber chickens, novelty candy). Known for visual puns and one-moment gags. The joke is over in 5 seconds. Cheap feeling. No extended experience. No connected universe. No comedy depth.
  • Pranko (Prank-O): Owns "funny fake product boxes." Realistic prank packaging that looks like an absurd product. The concept is clever, but the actual copy is weak, the boxes are empty inside, not durable, and the humor doesn't sustain past the initial reveal. One-trick pony.
  • Vat19: Owns "cool novelty products with great video content." Strong YouTube presence (billions of views). Products are interesting and giftable but more "cool/weird" than "hilarious." The joke is the product itself — once you see it, the comedy is done. More of a novelty retailer than a comedy brand.
  • Generic Amazon gag gifts (Gagster, etc.): Own nothing — they're commodity sellers competing on price and Amazon SEO. Cheap quality, shallow humor, no brand loyalty, disposable.

Indirect Competitors

  • Gift cards (the "I give up" option that our buyer is trying to avoid).
  • Experience gifts (comedy show tickets, escape rooms — competing for the same "fun gift" intent).
  • Funny printed products (mugs, socks, t-shirts with jokes — functional items with surface humor).
  • Off the Wagon Shop: Curated novelty retailer, not a brand. Resells other brands' products.

Positioning Map

  • X-axis: Humor depth (shallow/one-moment → deep/extended experience)
  • Y-axis: Product quality (cheap/disposable → premium/keepable)
  • Archie McPhee: Low-left (shallow humor, cheap quality)
  • Pranko: Mid-left (clever concept but shallow execution, cheap quality)
  • Vat19: Mid-center (moderate humor, decent quality, but comedy doesn't sustain)
  • Generic Amazon sellers: Bottom-left (shallowest humor, cheapest quality)
  • Wittyverse: Top-right (deepest humor, highest quality) — this quadrant is empty.

White Space

  • No brand in the gag gift category occupies "premium extended comedy experience." Every competitor delivers a moment. Wittyverse delivers an experience.
  • No competitor sources from a real parallel dimension. The Wittyverse is a structural moat: competitors can copy any individual product, but they can't access the catalog. The advantage compounds with every launch.

Category Conventions (what's "normal" in this space)

  • Products are cheap ($5-15), disposable, and deliver a single joke.
  • Packaging is an afterthought, not part of the experience.
  • Humor is surface-level — puns, visual gags, one-liners.
  • No brand loyalty — customers buy whatever Amazon recommends.
  • No content ecosystem — products exist in isolation. Points of Parity vs. Points of Difference

Points of Parity (Table Stakes)

  • Must be giftable — easy to buy, wrap, and give for birthdays, holidays, White Elephant, Secret Santa.
  • Must be funny on first impression — the product concept itself needs to land immediately, before anyone reads the fine print.
  • Must be available on Amazon — this is where the category lives and where buyers default to searching.
  • Must be priced within the gift-giving comfort zone — not so expensive it feels risky for a joke gift.
  • Must photograph well — the product needs to look good in a listing image and in someone's hands.

Points of Difference (Where We Stand Apart)

  • Extended entertainment vs. one-moment gag. 5-10 minutes of active group laughter vs. the category standard of 5 seconds.
  • Joke density vs. single joke. Every square inch — packaging, fine print, instructions, warnings — contains humor. Competitors leave most surfaces blank.
  • Museum-quality design vs. cheap novelty. Professional design that looks beautiful even while being absurd. Display-worthy, not disposable.
  • Real, interconnected universe vs. disconnected one-offs. The Wittyverse — a parallel dimension we source from — connects every product, creating Easter eggs, cross-references, and a deeper world that rewards repeat visits. No competitor has anything like this, and they can't replicate it: the catalog isn't ours to copyright, it's ours to import.
  • Engineered group experience vs. solo consumption. Products are designed to be read aloud, explored together, and shared — creating a social event, not just a gift.
  • Keeper status vs. trash-can destiny. Built with durable materials and enough depth that recipients display, save, and re-show products to friends who weren't there.

§5 Brand Values

Value 1: Density Over Breadth

Definition

  • We'd rather pack 50 jokes into one product than launch 50 products with one joke each.

Trade-off Pair

  • We choose joke density over product velocity. This means slower launch cycles and fewer SKUs than competitors.

Decision Scenario(s)

  • A product developer has an idea for a new gag gift. It's clever — a solid concept — but after a full design pass, it only sustains about 90 seconds of engagement. Decision: Kill it or rework it. If it can't hold a group for 5-10 minutes, it doesn't ship, even if it would sell.
  • The team is debating whether to launch 3 new products this quarter or invest the same resources into making 1 product exceptional. Decision: Make 1 exceptional product. One product that becomes legendary is worth more than three that are "pretty good." Value 2: Craft Over Shock

Definition

  • The humor must be engineered, not just edgy. A fart joke that's really about molecular physics is on brand. A fart joke that's just a fart joke is not.

Trade-off Pair

  • We choose smart execution over easy laughs. This means some jokes take longer to land, and some audiences won't get them.

Decision Scenario(s)

  • A copywriter pitches a product joke that's undeniably funny but relies entirely on shock value — it's crude and one-dimensional. Decision: Rewrite it. The subject can be crude; the execution must be clever.
  • A social media post gets huge engagement with a simple meme that has nothing to do with the Wittyverse or brand voice. Decision: Don't chase it. Viral moments that dilute the brand are net negative long-term. Value 3: Experience Over Product

Definition

  • We sell 10 minutes of shared laughter, not a physical object. The object is just the delivery mechanism.

Trade-off Pair

  • We choose experience design over cost optimization. This means higher COGS (better materials, more print surfaces, packaging-as-product) and lower margins per unit.

Decision Scenario(s)

  • Manufacturing suggests we can save $0.80/unit by using thinner cardstock for interior pages. It would still look "fine." Decision: Keep the premium stock. The product needs to feel substantial in hand — "fine" doesn't create keeper status.
  • A new product concept works great as a standalone item but doesn't naturally involve group participation. Decision: Redesign for social. If it can't be read aloud or explored as a group, it's missing the core value proposition. Value 4: Universe Over One-Offs

Definition

  • Every product, every piece of content, every joke builds the Wittyverse. Nothing exists in isolation.

Trade-off Pair

  • We choose long-term worldbuilding over short-term convenience. This means more creative constraints and longer development cycles, but a compounding asset that competitors cannot replicate.

Decision Scenario(s)

  • A product idea is hilarious but doesn't fit within Wittyverse logic. Decision: Either adapt it to fit the universe or don't make it. The Wittyverse is the moat. Exceptions erode it.
  • A content creator wants to reference a real-world brand for a joke. Decision: Invent a Wittyverse equivalent. It's harder, but it builds canon and keeps the world intact. Value 5: Warmth Over Edge

Definition

  • Everyone laughs. Nobody gets hurt. The Wittyverse is a good place — weird, but good.

Trade-off Pair

  • We choose inclusive laughter over boundary-pushing edge. This means walking away from humor that's funny to some people at the expense of others.

Decision Scenario(s)

  • A joke is genuinely hilarious to the team but could make a recipient feel mocked rather than included. Decision: Cut it. If the gift recipient isn't laughing too, we failed.
  • A product concept flirts with a culture-war topic that would generate buzz. Decision: Hard no. The Wittyverse is an escape from the real world, not a mirror of its problems. No politics, no religion, no culture war. Ever.

§6 Brand Personality

Personality Traits (with behavioral guardrails)

  1. Wickedly Clever
  • Is: The person who drops a joke so layered that you laugh immediately, then laugh again 10 seconds later when you catch the second meaning. Commands a room through wit, not volume.
  • Is not: Pretentious, inaccessible, or "smarter than you" energy. The humor never requires a decoder ring — the surface joke always lands, the deeper layers are bonuses.
  • Guardrail: If a joke needs to be explained, rewrite it. The surface must work for everyone; the depth rewards the attentive.
  1. Unflappably Deadpan
  • Is: Treats the most absurd situations with complete sincerity. A child-launching device has a product recall? Describe it like a Toyota recall. The comedy comes from the gap between delivery and content.
  • Is not: Sarcastic, winking, or self-aware. Never breaks character. Never says "just kidding." Never acknowledges the absurdity.
  • Guardrail: If any content includes an exclamation mark of excitement, a reaction of amazement to weirdness, or a "can you believe this?" aside — rewrite.
  1. Generous With Joy
  • Is: The person who brings the best gift to every party — not to show off, but because they genuinely love watching other people laugh. The brand exists to create shared moments of happiness.
  • Is not: Sentimental, saccharine, or performatively warm. The warmth is always in the subtext, never the text.
  • Guardrail: If a piece of content or product could make someone feel excluded, targeted, or laughed at rather than laughed with — kill it.
  1. Obsessively Crafted
  • Is: The person who spent 40 hours on a detail most people will never notice — and considers it time well spent. Quality is visible in every square inch: the joke in the fine print, the Easter egg on the back panel, the packaging that's as entertaining as what's inside.
  • Is not: Precious or perfectionist to the point of paralysis. Ships excellent work, not perfect work.
  • Guardrail: If any surface of a product has empty space that could contain a joke, it's not done.
  1. Mischievous (Not Malicious)
  • Is: The person who orchestrates an elaborate, harmless prank that leaves even the target laughing. Pushes envelopes; never crosses lines. "Edgy" in the sense of clever boundary-pushing, not in the sense of cruelty.
  • Is not: Mean-spirited, bullying, punching down, or using shock as a substitute for craft.
  • Guardrail: Apply the "would the recipient laugh too?" test to everything. If the answer is anything less than "yes, absolutely" — rethink.

The Party Test

  • If Wittyverse walked into a party, he'd be the guy everyone is glad showed up — not because he's the loudest, but because he always brings something no one has seen before that makes the whole room lose it. He's impossibly quick-witted, dressed like he doesn't care but somehow looks great, and has a prank at the ready that's so elaborate and funny you can't even be mad. He's the funniest person in the room, and possibly one of the smartest, but he'd never let you feel that gap. He makes you funnier by proximity — he sets up the joke, and you get to deliver it when you give someone his product.

What This Brand Would Never Do or Say

  • Never punch down — no humor at the expense of marginalized groups, individuals, or people who can't fight back.
  • Never break the Wittyverse's fourth wall in content — the universe is real. Period.
  • Never use hype language: "OMG," "insane," "epic," "mind-blowing," "you won't believe."
  • Never use internet slang it didn't earn: "bestie," "no cap," "slay," "it's giving."
  • Never touch politics, religion, culture war, or real-world controversy.
  • Never release a product that's "good enough" — if it doesn't pass all 10 quality gates, it doesn't ship.
  • Never sacrifice the joke for the sale — if content feels like an ad, it's already failed.
  • Never be boring. This is the cardinal sin.

Voice & Tone Direction Seeds

  • Written content (Wittyverse): Deadpan, specific, grounded in relatable emotions, committed to the premise, escalating. Think: The Onion's structural discipline meets Hitchhiker's Guide bureaucratic absurdism in an Omega Mart universe.
  • Product copy: In-universe, joke-dense, every surface covered, Wittyverse-native language. Overpromising + legal disclaimers + casual warnings.
  • Customer-facing (non-Wittyverse): Witty but warm, clever but accessible, playful yet professional, mischievous but trustworthy. Never salesy, never boring.
  • Ratio: 80% smart / 20% silly. Smart sets the table; silly delivers the punchline. On-Brand / Off-Brand Voice Samples Note: These samples validate that the personality traits are specific enough to produce consistent output. They are NOT a voice guide — the full voice and messaging system is developed in its own dedicated solving run.

On-Brand Examples

  • Headline: "The Office of Operational Product Standards has issued a recall on the Child Chucker Model WY-69 following reports of 'unexpected trajectory deviation.'"

    • Why it works: Deadpan, specific (model number, official language), treats the absurd as mundane. The humor is in the gap between the clinical delivery and the insane content.

    • Product description snippet: "Scientifically engineered to contain absolutely nothing. Just add water to reconstitute. Patent office responded to our original filing with 'lol.'"

      • Why it works: Committed to the bit (scientifically engineered nothing), specific detail (patent office response), layered humor (the paradox of adding water to make water). Never winks.

      • Social caption: "Reminder: Summoned entities must be dismissed before end of business day. Your summoning, your problem."

        • Why it works: In-universe, treats magic as an HR compliance issue, deadpan tone, no exclamation marks, no hype. The funniest word is "Reminder" because it implies this keeps happening.

Off-Brand Examples (What Wittyverse Would NEVER Sound Like)

     * Headline: "OMG You NEED to See This Insane Gag Gift!! 🤣🤣🤣"

        * Why it fails: Hype language, exclamation marks, emoji, "OMG" — breaks every deadpan rule. Sounds like an excited social media manager, not a brand with confidence.
        * Product description snippet: "This hilarious prank will have your friends DYING of laughter! Perfect for anyone who loves a good joke! Great for parties!"

           * Why it fails: Generic (could describe any product), salesy, uses excitement as a substitute for actual humor. No specificity, no commitment to a premise, no craft.
           * Social caption: "Who's ready for some Monday mischief?? Drop a 😈 if you love pranks! #PrankLife #GagGifts #FunnyAF"

              * Why it fails: "Fellow kids" energy, hashtag spam, asks for engagement instead of earning it, uses slang the brand didn't earn. Content is about the brand, not from the Wittyverse.

§7 Brand Mantra

The Mantra

              * Shockingly good laughter.

How to Use It

              * A product concept hits your desk: "Is this shockingly good? Would someone be genuinely shocked that a gag gift is this well-made, this funny, this dense?" If not, keep going.
              * A social post draft is ready: "Does this deliver laughter, or just information? Does the quality of the humor shock — is it better than people expect from a brand?" If not, rewrite.
              * A customer service interaction: "Will this person walk away shocked by how good the experience was?" If not, go further.
              * The mantra captures the two-part promise: the quality shocks (because the category has trained people to expect garbage) and the output is laughter (not just smiles, not just chuckles — genuine, room-filling laughter).

North Star (Johnny verbatim)

  • "Would this product make someone's friends say: 'Hot damn. That is amazing. Where did you GET that?'" — Johnny Lasater, Product Vision Manifesto, 11/08/25

How They Work Together

  • Mantra ("Shockingly good laughter") = internal compass for decision-making. The bar.
  • North Star ("Hot damn..." reaction) = target outcome from the customer. The proof of bar-met.
  • A product/post/interaction that hits the mantra creates the North Star reaction.

§8 Brand-Customer Relationship

The Relationship Metaphor

  • The brand–customer relationship rests on one promise: the customer becomes the party hero. Everything else flows from that.
  • Wittyverse is the co-conspirator that makes that happen. Not a friend, not a guru, not a service provider — a co-conspirator. Wittyverse and the customer are in on it together: the mission is to make the gift recipient (and the whole room) lose it, and the gift-giver the party hero. The customer brings the occasion and the audience; Wittyverse brings the material. When it lands, the customer gets the credit. Wittyverse is the ghostwriter of the party's best moment.

What This Means Functionally

              * The customer is the hero, not the brand. Wittyverse never takes center stage. The gift-giver takes the bow. Every product, every piece of packaging, every joke is engineered to make the customer look clever, generous, and hilarious — not to make the brand look impressive. The brand succeeds when someone says "where did you GET that?" not "oh, that came from Wittyverse."
              * The relationship is low-maintenance, high-reward. The buyer doesn't want to work hard to find the right gift. Wittyverse is the easy button — browse, pick one, done. Confidence that it'll land is the core promise. The brand earns repeat visits by delivering every time, not by demanding loyalty or engagement.
              * Trust is built through product, not marketing. The customer is skeptical of brands trying to be their friend. Wittyverse earns trust the same way a great restaurant does — by being undeniably good every time, not by running ads about how good it is.

How This Guides Tone and Interactions

              * Customer-facing tone: Confident, not desperate. Witty, not salesy. The energy of someone who knows they have the best thing in the room and doesn't need to convince you — you'll see for yourself.
              * Marketing implication: Content should feel like something the customer discovered, not something that was pushed at them. The Wittyverse content ecosystem is the primary vehicle — people engage because it's genuinely entertaining, and the products live naturally inside that world.
              * Customer service implication: When something goes wrong, the co-conspirator energy means: "We messed up your mission. Let's fix it immediately so you can still be the hero." Speed, warmth, and over-delivery — never corporate, never scripted.

§9 Origin Narrative

Narrative Archetype

              * The Industry Insider Who Got Fed Up — a serial entrepreneur who saw that the gag gift market was dominated by cheap, lazy, forgettable junk and decided to prove it could be done at a level nobody thought possible.

The Story

              * Johnny Lasater has been building businesses since he was 11 — mowing lawns in Boulder, CO, detailing cars, flipping products on Craigslist, eventually building JLS Trading Co. into a multi-brand ecommerce company in Charlotte, NC. In 2016, he launched Witty Yeti's first product — Bad Parking Cards — and something clicked. The gag gift space was wide open: every competitor was selling cheap, shallow, forgettable novelties. One joke, five seconds of laughter, then the trash can. Johnny saw a category full of products that were, in his words, "shit" — and decided to make ones that weren't. The thesis was simple: what if a gag gift was so densely packed with jokes, so beautifully designed, and so ridiculously entertaining that people kept it? What if the gift-giver became the hero of the party? What if a $20 product delivered a better comedy experience than a $100 night out? That obsession — making gag gifts that are shockingly better than anything else in the category — became Witty Yeti.

Why This Story Is Credible

              * Johnny's serial entrepreneurship and ecommerce infrastructure (JLS Trading Co. with multiple brands, warehousing, supply chain) prove operational capability.
              * The product quality is demonstrably superior — joke density, design quality, and physical construction are visibly better than any competitor's, verifiable by picking up any Witty Yeti product.
              * The Wittyverse (a parallel dimension connecting all products) is a structural innovation no competitor has attempted, let alone built.

9b. Universe-Side Origin (Winston's Discovery) Audited 2026-05-13. Internal canon only — storefront copy is governed by Location & Identity Mystery (see voice rules below). Storefront/About NEVER names: NC, Winston's real surname, the portal's location, the cave, or the Himalayas. Storefront CAN name: Winston, "Dr. W. Yeti PhD," the Wittyverse.

Narrative Archetype (Internal)

              * The Treasure-Hunter Who Tripped Into Another Dimension — a slightly-shady Earth-born quick-buck operator who went looking for a rumored creature and found a parallel universe instead.

The Story (Internal Canon)

              * Winston is Earth-born. A treasure-hunter / "looking for a quick buck" type — slightly shady but not really. While treasure-hunting in the Himalayas (chasing gold, lost treasure, and a rumored yeti), he found a vehicle-sized portal inside a cave. He crossed through, discovered the Wittyverse (a real parallel dimension), and recognized its absurd products as commercial gag-gift opportunities back on Earth. He started importing. The portal is trivially crossable — Winston comes and goes as he pleases — and he keeps its location secret to protect his monopoly. He named the dimension "Wittyverse" after himself (his nickname is "Witty" — narcissist move). He renamed himself "Yeti" after the creature he'd been hunting; his real surname is unspecified. Sentence Structure Rules

Length and Rhythm

              * Default sentence length: 8–18 words. Short enough to scan, long enough for personality.
              * Mix it up. Follow a longer sentence with a short punch. "Every surface is packed with jokes — front, back, inside, fine print, all of it. You'll be reading for ten minutes. That's not an exaggeration."
              * One idea per sentence. If a sentence has a comma followed by a new idea, split it.
              * The power position is the end of the sentence. Put the most important word or the punchline last. "Most gag gifts are funny for five seconds. This one's funny for ten minutes." Not: "For ten minutes, this one is funny."

Paragraph Rules

              * Three sentences max per paragraph in digital contexts (web, email, social). Dense paragraphs kill readability.
              * Lead with the claim, follow with the proof. Not the reverse. "Every surface is packed with jokes. Front, back, inside, packaging, fine print — all of it."
              * End paragraphs on strong beats. Never trail off with filler ("...and much more!" / "...and so much else!").

Active Voice

              * Always active, never passive. "We designed every surface" not "Every surface was designed."
              * Exception: Deadpan humor sometimes uses passive for comic effect. "Side effects have been reported." This is intentional voice, not lazy construction.

Questions

              * Use sparingly. One question per page/email max. Questions work as section openers ("Ever given a gag gift that held a room for ten minutes?") but become gimmicky in clusters.
              * Never rhetorical hype questions. "Ready for the funniest gift EVER?!" — no.
              * Allowed: Genuine, slightly provocative questions. "When's the last time a gag gift made you want to keep it?"

Lists and Bullets

              * Bullets are for scannability, not personality. Keep bullet copy tight and parallel. Personality lives in the intro sentence before the list.
              * Amazon exception: Bullets are the primary copy format. Each bullet should lead with a benefit and follow with proof. Personality is compressed into word choice and rhythm, not structure.

Tone Marker Rules

These are the small signals that make writing feel like Wittyverse and not like any other brand.

Contractions: Yes, Always

              * "We're" not "We are." "You'll" not "You will." "It's" not "It is."
              * Exception: Omit contractions for deliberate emphasis. "This is not a cheap gag gift." The full "is not" adds weight.

Direct Address: "You" Over Everything

              * Always "you" — never "our customers," "gift-givers," "shoppers," or "one."
              * "You'll be laughing for ten minutes." Not "Customers report ten minutes of laughter."

Exclamation Marks: Near-Zero

              * Maximum one per full page of copy. And only when genuine excitement is warranted (e.g., a major product launch).
              * Zero in product descriptions, transactional emails, or customer service. The confidence of the brand comes from the absence of exclamation marks. The copy is funny. It doesn't need to tell you it's funny.
              * Social media: One per post max, and only if the rhythm demands it.

Humor Density in Customer-Facing Copy

              * Not every line needs a joke. Unlike in-universe copy (where every square inch must contain humor), the customer-facing voice uses humor as seasoning.
              * Rule of thumb: One genuinely clever moment per content block (paragraph, email section, product listing bullet cluster). The rest should be clear, confident, well-written prose with personality in the rhythm and word choice.
              * The humor should feel effortless. If a joke feels forced or inserted, cut it. The voice should sound like a naturally funny person writing clearly — not a copywriter inserting humor beats.

Punctuation as Personality

              * Em dashes are a signature move. Use for asides, pivots, and punchlines. "Every surface is packed with jokes — even the parts you'd normally skip."
              * Periods for punch. Short declarative sentences ending in periods create confidence. "Most gag gifts are funny for five seconds. This one isn't."
              * Ellipses: never. They create uncertainty. This brand doesn't trail off.
              * Semicolons: rarely. They're formal. Use em dashes or periods instead.

4B. Naming Principles

Every new name must pass all seven. No exceptions.

  1. Pronounceable on first read If someone can't say it out loud confidently after seeing it once, it fails. Gag gifts are read aloud — names must work as spoken words.
  2. Three words maximum Product names: 1–3 words. Entity names: 1–4 words (acronyms count as one word). If it needs more than three words, it's a description, not a name.
  3. Sounds like it belongs in the Wittyverse Product and entity names should feel like they were invented by someone inside the parallel dimension. They should have the deadpan, slightly-off quality of Wittyverse language — mundane words applied to absurd things, or bureaucratic precision about nonsensical subjects.
  4. Funny or intriguing on its own The name itself should provoke a reaction — a raised eyebrow, a slight smile, a "what is that?" Even without context, the name should pull you in.
  5. No real-world brand references Per Wittyverse rules: the universe doesn't know our world exists. No parodies of real brand names, no references to real companies, agencies, or products. Invent the Wittyverse equivalent.
  6. Works in a sentence Test: can you naturally say "I got you [product name]" or "Have you seen [product name]"? If the name is awkward in conversation, it fails. This is critical — these products are talked about at parties.
  7. Not already taken Trademark search, Amazon search, domain check, social handle check. Doesn't need to be a unique trademark class filing for every product — but it can't already be someone else's thing.

4C. Naming Taxonomy

Products

                    * Style: Wittyverse-native proper names. Named as the product would be named inside the universe by its in-universe creator or regulating body.
                    * Capitalization: Title Case (proper noun).
                    * Tone: Deadpan, specific, slightly absurd. The name should sound official or mundane while describing something ridiculous.
                    * Pattern guidance: Favor names that sound like they belong on a shelf, in a catalog, or in a regulatory filing inside the Wittyverse. "Dehydrated Water" is better than "Funny Water Kit." "Child Chucker" is better than "Kid Launcher LOL."

Wittyverse Entities (agencies, characters, locations, companies)

                    * Acronyms allowed when: (a) the full name is funnier than the acronym, (b) the acronym itself forms a word or near-word (O.O.P.S.), and (c) the full name is always available for the reader to discover.

3. Voice Attributes

Deadpan The writer treats everything with complete seriousness. The comedy comes from the content, not the delivery. A child being launched into a residential pond by a malfunctioning spring-loaded device is described with the same clinical precision as a product recall notice — because in the Wittyverse, that's exactly what it is. Much of the humor lives in the gap between how seriously the writer treats the subject and how absurd the subject actually is. But deadpan doesn't mean humorless — the writer is still actively funny through wordplay, clever turns of phrase, and perfectly placed punchlines. The writer's wit operates within the world's logic, never stepping outside it. The test: If your line would be funnier with a winky face or an "lol" — delete those and see if it still works. If it does, you've got deadpan. If it collapses without them, you haven't committed to the bit yet. Specific Vague absurdism is not funny. Specific absurdism is. "Weird side effects" gets nothing. "May cause temporary disembodiment or mild timeline splitting, usually fading within 4–6 hours" gets a screenshot sent to a friend. Every piece should include model numbers, regulatory codes, dosage limits, filing deadlines, complaint reference numbers, or similar bureaucratic specifics — applied to impossible things. Grounded Every piece starts from a real human experience — relationship frustration, workplace annoyance, consumer complaints, parenting exhaustion. The absurd cause is revealed after the emotional hook is set. The pattern: relatable emotion → absurd cause → mundane treatment of the absurd cause. The Wittyverse Substitution Rule: When a piece calls for a relatable scenario, the emotional function should be instantly recognizable, but the specific detail should be Wittyverse-native. "I needed water for a hurricane" is lazy — the reader is still in our world. "I needed water to wait out the gravitational inversion" does the same emotional work while pulling the reader fully into the Wittyverse. Committed Half-committed absurdism reads as random. If the premise is that a boyfriend is a gaseous lifeform, the advice column must discuss molecular cohesion, baseline density, the social etiquette of maintaining form in public, and the political correctness of the word "gassy" versus "gaseous." Commit through language, not just premise. It's not enough for the situation to be absurd — the vocabulary has to live inside the absurdity too. "Entirely fabricated" is fine, but "a gross misrepresentation of our dehydration pipeline" is committed. Every time you reach for a generic word when a specific, absurd, in-universe word could go there instead, you're leaving a joke on the table. Warm (in the Background) The Wittyverse is fundamentally a good place — and this is a guardrail, not a suggestion. The world is thriving, not collapsing. People are trying their best. The bureaucrats mean well even if they're useless. Life is weird, but life is good. This warmth is never the text — it's the subtext. You'll never see a character say "isn't life wonderful?" If a line can be funny without sacrificing clarity or momentum, make it funny. If making it funny would break the rhythm, leave it clean. What's Funny to This Brand Bureaucratic absurdism.                     * Shock without craft. If the only reason something is "funny" is that it's offensive or surprising, it doesn't belong.

Escalation Building humor by increasing absurdity or stakes. Non-negotiable in the Wittyverse. Leave your strongest punchline for the end. A piece that peaks in paragraph two is a failed piece. Callback Referencing an earlier joke later for additional humor. Running gags are callbacks used across multiple pieces — valuable for building canon and the "inside joke" feeling. Never point out the irony — let the reader feel clever for noticing it. Hyperbole In the Wittyverse, exaggerate within the world's own logic — push the established absurdity further. "The detonation radius has been described as 'impressive' by three structural engineers and 'oh my God' by the rest." Parody The Wittyverse's primary structural device. Every piece is a parody of a real-world format — advice columns, PSAs, news articles. The closer you adhere to the format's real conventions, the funnier the absurd content becomes. Satire Targets systems and culture, not people: consumer culture, bureaucratic incompetence, regulatory theater, corporate euphemism, liability paranoia, advertising confidence. Non Sequitur Use sparingly — humor is generally logical within its own framework. Effective for: contact information that glitches into nonsense, fine print that derails, parenthetical asides that wander. "Contact your regional O.O.P.S. office between the hours of 9 AM and the concept of Thursday." The Power of Two Comparing/contrasting two items for a one-two punch. "Not flushable. Not forgettable." / "The product performed as intended. The user did not."

5. Anti-Voice & Banned Content

Wittyverse Content Never Sounds Like... * A brand trying to sell you something. Product references feel natural — a journalist mentioning a product, not a copywriter plugging it. * A comedian doing a set. The writer is a journalist, columnist, or official who happens to be sharp. The comedy is a byproduct of covering an insane beat. * An excited social media manager. No hype, no breathlessness, no "You guys, you NEED to see this." * A college humor sketch. The brand has matured. 2015 humor doesn't land in 2026. * An insider winking at the audience. Never break the fourth wall. Never acknowledge the Wittyverse is not real. Never reference our world. 6. Drift Patterns: Common R1 Mistakes

The Excitement Leak Copy starts with infomercial conviction, then gets excited about itself. * ❌ "The Child Chucker. Rated 4.2 stars — WE LOVE THIS PRODUCT SO MUCH 🔥" * Fix: Read every line as if delivering to camera at 2am on a shopping channel. The Wink The brand signals to the audience that it knows this is all a joke. * ❌ "Dehydrated Water — yes, it's a real product lol." * Fix: Delete every acknowledgment that something is weird, funny, or intentional. The Half-Commit Copy gets 70% there and softens the landing. * ❌ "Results may vary. Yours probably will. But hey, worth a try!" * Fix: Stop one sentence earlier. "Results may vary. Yours probably will." is the complete thought. The Emoji Substitute Emoji doing the work the copy should be doing. * ❌ "New arrival 🔥🔥🔥" * ✅ "New arrival. The Reality Stabilizer. The Explanation Telling the audience why something is funny. * ❌ "The Child Chucker is funny because it sounds like a dangerous parenting product but it's actually just for timeouts!" * Fix: Delete the explanation entirely. 7. Format-Specific Registers

Advice Columns ("Dear Dimensional") Register: Warm, blunt, slightly exasperated expertise. House MD meets Dear Abby. What shifts: Most emotionally warm format. The column's voice genuinely cares, even when roasting. Runs unattributed — the byline is "Dear Dimensional," not a named columnist.

Structure

                    * Reader letter (200–300 words): Relatable problem → Wittyverse reveal creeps in → treated as normal
                    * Response (200–300 words): Acknowledges real emotional problem → practical advice within Wittyverse logic → worldbuilding details → punchy closer
                    * P.S.: One final joke or worldbuilding detail. Often the hardest-hitting beat.

Tone calibration

                    * Too cold: "Your boyfriend is a gaseous lifeform. Deal with it."
                    * Just right: "The fact that he feels comfortable enough to fully lose cohesion around you? That's actually sweet."
                    * Too warm: "Oh honey, I totally understand how hard interspecies relationships can be!"

PSAs / Safety Advisories (O.O.P.S.) Register: Bureaucratic, clinical, emotionless. Zero personality. Comedy comes entirely from contrast between flat procedural language and absurd content. What shifts: Least emotional format. No warmth, no wit from the writer, no personality. The "writer" is an institution.

Structure

                    * Incident summary with clinical precision (model numbers, weights, distances)
                    * Statistical context (how common, prior incidents)
                    * Official consumer guidance in flattest possible language
                    * Contact information that subtly breaks (glitch text, impossible phone numbers)

Tone calibration

                    * Too human: "We're concerned about the rising number of incidents."
                    * Just right: "This marks the fourth such incident this quarterly cycle."
                    * Too comedic: "Another kid went flying — classic!"

News Articles (Journalism) Register: Neutral, professional, straight journalism. The reporter is invisible — just facts, quotes, and context. What shifts: No personality, no warmth, no exasperation — just journalistic conventions.

Structure follows real journalism

                    * Lede: What happened, stated plainly
                    * Nut graf: Why it matters, context
                    * Quotes from sources
                    * Background and history
                    * What happens next

Tone calibration

                    * Too editorialized: "In yet another baffling incident..."
                    * Just right: "Authorities confirmed Tuesday that the recall now affects an estimated 3.2 million vehicles."
                    * Too dry: If the content isn't absurd enough to carry the piece, pick a better premise.

Critical: Every article must work for a reader with zero prior context. The article should organically establish what the product is through reporting. A piece that assumes familiarity becomes an inside joke, and inside jokes don't get screenshotted and sent to friends. Product Copy (Packaging, Instructions, Warnings, Fine Print) Register: The deadpan infomercial host at maximum commitment. Overpromising + legal disclaimers + casual warnings. This is where "only legal requirements break character; otherwise 100% in-universe" applies absolutely.

Rules

                    * Every square inch contains jokes — front, back, inside, packaging, fine print
                    * In-universe branding, warnings, instructions, testimonials
                    * Light references to Wittyverse elements (not deep dives)
                    * Cross-references to other products for worldbuilding and cross-marketing
                    * Products are real, claims are sincere, absurdity is never acknowledged

Wittyverse Storefront Toggle (Website) Register: Products sold as Wittyverse locals would see them. Complete sincerity. No "gag gift" framing. No acknowledgment that anything is funny.

Rules

                    * Full R1 voice — Codex governs every element
                    * Product descriptions are functional, clinical, deadpan
                    * Navigation, CTAs, and page copy should feel like a store that exists inside the Wittyverse
                    * Checkout, customer service, and returns stay R2 (handled by the superprompt, not this attachment)

Example product page: "Dehydrated Water. Shelf-stable. Lightweight. Rated compliant by the Office of Operational Product Standards under Directive 9.1(c). Add water to reconstitute. Do not exceed recommended water-to-water ratio. Available in single-serve and 12-pack."

The Five Faces The Wittyverse type system is five typefaces, each locked to a single role. No face crosses into another role. (Fifth face — Bowlby One as secondary "pulp display" — added 2026-05-15.) Face Role Spec Lilita One THE WORDMARK FACE. Used only for the logo wordmark (WITTYVERSE) and the W monogram. Never body, never display, never headline copy. All-caps WITTYVERSE only. Single weight (Lilita One ships only weight 400). Locked treatment: transform: skewX(-6deg); text-shadow: 4px 4px 0 var(--ink), 8px 8px 0 var(--lime) (canonical orange-on-dark variant); orange fill, ink mid-shadow, lime back-shadow. Monogram uses the same letterform at −5° skew with the identical stack. (Stack updated 2026-05-15 from orange-deep back-shadow to lime to match the W monogram's color treatment — pulp/showman register sharpens with the high-contrast complementary back-stop.) Big Shoulders Display SITE DISPLAY. Used for hero copy, section headings, subheadings, buttons. NEVER for the wordmark, NEVER for the pulp-display moment (use Bowlby One). Weight 900 (regular, not italic). Slant comes from CSS transform: skewX(...), not from the typeface italic — italic + skew compounds to ~16° of slant which loses the pulp-poster punch and reads "tilted, not engineered". Display headlines tolerate −5° to −7° skew; the wordmark slot is reserved for Lilita One. (Corrected 2026-05-15: prior bible said "900 italic", was a mistake — the italic variant was being layered ON TOP of the CSS skew unintentionally.) Bowlby One PULP DISPLAY. Reserved for the single loudest moment on a page — hero banner, marquee strip, social-ad headline, retail-poster moment. Maximum ONE use per page. NEVER for the wordmark, body, eyebrows, navigation, or CTAs. Weight 400 (Bowlby One ships only weight 400). Locked treatment: transform: skewX(-7deg); same stacked-shadow stack as the wordmark — orange fill, ink mid-shadow, lime back-shadow. Pairs as a poster sibling to Big Shoulders Display: BSD does the rest of the page's headlines; Bowlby does the one moment that has to land hardest. Added 2026-05-15 (Phase A.7 follow-up — the v18-the-pulp prototype proved the Bowlby+BSD poster pairing reads as one family at different scales). Public Sans BODY. Used for all running text, navigation, UI labels, prose, and kicker lines. Weights 400 / 500 / 600 / 700 / 800. Kicker text (e.g., "Operated by Winston, Dr. W. Yeti PhD") uses Public Sans italic at 11–13px. (Body face swapped from Inter to Public Sans on 2026-05-15 — civic-tech humanist register matches Winston's deadpan "authorized importer / customs broker" framing better than Inter's SaaS-default neutrality. Public Sans is designed by the US Web Design System team for federal websites; its institutional-trustworthy voice quietly reinforces the customs-paperwork joke at body size. Inter retained as system fallback during font load.) JetBrains Mono DEEP-CANON TAG. Used for the mono "CYCLE 247" tag, regulatory microline, mono badges, and any artifact that bridges the deadpan-world (R1) register into the brand layer. 10–12px, all-caps, 0.12em letter-spacing. IBM Plex Mono and Space Mono are sanctioned swaps if availability demands. Wordmark casing lock — ALL CAPS The wordmark is always rendered ALL CAPS as WITTYVERSE, never as mixed-case Wittyverse. Locked 2026-05-11 after the type-stack-pairings test confirmed: * Uniform cap-height creates one solid horizontal mass that the stacked color shadow can grip; mixed-case produces an uneven silhouette that flattens the shadow stack. * In the monogram chip, all-caps WITTYVERSE fills the circular ground authoritatively; mixed-case reads as timid by contrast. * All-caps matches Winston's importer-showman persona — reads as stamp / seal / catalog header / customs-dock marquee. Mixed-case reads as conversational body voice. The casing applies to every wordmark variant — orange-on-dark, white-on-dark, orange-on-cream, single-color ink, reversed white. The W monogram is a single glyph and is unaffected. Why Lilita One as the wordmark face Lilita One reads as showman / candy-bar / theme-park signage — which maps directly to Winston the Earth-side importer-showman selling parallel-dimension cargo. Across the converged top-3 design-sprint favorites (v13 Candybar, v17 Remixed Dark Grid, v18 The Pulp), Lilita One (or its near-twin Bagel Fat One in v13) was already doing the heaviest brand-identity work. The wordmark needs to sound like the brand already sounds. Phase A.7 re-research (2026-05-11) surveyed 17+ candidates across Google Fonts, Velvetyne, Pangram Pangram, OHno Type Co, Hoodzpah, Pizza Typefaces, Latinotype, and curated picks from 1001fonts and Adobe Fonts. Cubano was rendered natively with the locked treatment for direct head-to-head comparison; a slab-serif detour was explored and rejected for vibe mismatch. Conclusion: no chunky-rounded face within free or mid-tier budget beats Lilita One on the vibe-AND-distinctiveness bar. The pulp-pop chunky-rounded register is confirmed as the right family. Big Shoulders Display, by contrast, reads as athletic / industrial / sport — wrong register for a wordmark. It stays as the site display face for hero copy and section headings (where its industrial-poster energy works) and is explicitly forbidden as the wordmark face (see incorrect-usage.md NEVER 3).
